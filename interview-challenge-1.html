The Challenge

The Interview Prompt

This was a take-home product challenge given during a PM interview at Dream11 - India's largest fantasy sports platform, with 200M+ users. The brief was open-ended: design an experimentation strategy that can support the company at scale. No constraints, no prescribed format, just a blank canvas.

I treated this the way I'd treat any real product problem - start from the user (in this case, the product teams who need to make decisions), understand the business context, and work backwards to a strategy with clear principles and a concrete roadmap.

What this challenge tests

Experimentation strategy sits at the intersection of product, engineering, analytics, and culture. A strong answer doesn't just describe A/B testing mechanics - it defines why the platform needs experimentation, what principles it must be built on, and how it scales over time.

The Google story

In 2009, Google ran an experiment testing 41 different shades of blue for a single toolbar element. The result? One shade outperformed the others by a measurable margin - and that single experiment generated an estimated $200M in additional ad revenue. Not from a new product. Not from a new feature. From a colour decision backed by data instead of opinion.

The hook sets up the core argument: the difference between "we think" and "we know" is worth hundreds of millions of dollars. And it compounds - every product decision made with data rather than gut is a decision made with higher confidence and lower risk.

Context

What This Platform Was Facing

To make the strategy concrete, I grounded it in the specific challenges facing a high-scale sports platform. Four forces were creating urgency:

⚡
Massive Scale
Millions of users across multiple sports, each with different engagement patterns and expectations. At this scale, even small product decisions have outsized impact - positive or negative.
🏆
Competitive Market
The sports tech space moves fast. Competitors iterate continuously, and leadership requires a systematic ability to innovate - not just ship features, but ship the right features quickly.
🎯
Critical Decisions
Feature decisions directly affect millions of engaged users. A bad rollout isn't just a metric drop - it's churn in a sticky, emotionally invested audience that expects a premium experience.
📊
Limited Data Capability
The existing analytics infrastructure couldn't support confident decision-making at speed. Teams were making calls with incomplete data - working hard but not necessarily working right.
The synthesis

Together, these four forces create a clear imperative: move from "we think" to "we know". Reduce the risk of shipping the wrong thing. Build products users genuinely love - not products that we think they'll love.

North Star

The Vision

Before the pillars, before the roadmap - a single statement that anchors all the decisions that follow:

Experimentation Vision

"Empowering teams to deliver the best customer experience - with confidence."

This isn't a mission statement. It's a constraint. Every platform decision - what to build first, what to measure, how to structure the team model - has to pass through this filter. Does it make teams more confident? Does it improve the customer experience? If not, it's a distraction.

Strategy

Three Non-Negotiable Pillars

A good experimentation platform isn't just a technical product. It's a change management challenge. Teams have to trust it enough to run experiments, be curious enough to act on the results, and be confident that what they're measuring is real. The three pillars address each of these.

Pillar 01
Simplicity
Experiments are easily configured and analysed by anyone on the team - not just analysts. A self-service platform removes the activation energy required to run an experiment, which is the single biggest predictor of whether teams actually run them.
Measured by: # of experiments conducted
Pillar 02
Reliability
Results should be trustworthy. The end goal is decision-making - and if teams don't trust the output of an experiment, they'll override it with gut feel anyway. Reliable results require robust randomization, correct statistical methodology, and honest confidence intervals.
Measured by: Validation rate*
*% of experiments whose results fall within predicted range
Pillar 03
Scalability
The platform must handle the full customer base without degrading experiment quality. At scale, you need to run many experiments concurrently without cross-contamination, without increasing infrastructure cost linearly, and without slowing down the product team.
Measured by: # of concurrent experiments possible
Why It Matters

The Business Case for Experimentation

Experimentation isn't a nice-to-have. It's the operating system for making better product decisions faster. Here's how the four core benefits compound over time:

🚀
Move Fast
→ Faster time to market
A validated experiment result is the fastest way to ship with confidence. "Good enough to test" beats "perfect before launch" - every time. The discipline is getting comfortable shipping the experiment, not the finished product.
🔁
Iterate with Purpose
→ More successful features
Test, learn, pivot or proceed - then test again. Iteration only has value if it's informed. An experiment that kills a hypothesis early is as valuable as one that confirms it; both save you from building the wrong thing.
⚙️
Resource Efficiency
→ More output, less waste
Engineering effort scales only once an idea is validated. Without this gate, teams build features to completion before discovering they don't move the needle - an expensive, demoralising pattern that slows velocity over time.
⚠️
Risk Reduction
→ Cut losses early
Experimentation cuts losses early when an idea is off-track. For a platform with millions of users, a bad full rollout isn't just a missed opportunity - it's churn, it's trust erosion, and it's a difficult recovery. An experiment limits exposure to a fraction of traffic.
Roadmap

NOW / NEXT / LATER

The three pillars define what the platform must be. The roadmap defines how to get there - in three phases that build on each other, each delivering value while laying the foundation for the next.

NOW
Experimentation MVP
  • Basic A/B testing with reliable randomization
  • Dashboard with key experiment metrics monitoring
  • Standardized experiment design templates
  • Integration with existing analytics infrastructure
  • Documentation and team training programs
NEXT
Enhanced Capabilities
  • Self-service platform - any team can run experiments
  • Advanced statistical methods for sophisticated testing
  • Real-time monitoring with alerting
  • Basic holdout groups to measure cumulative impact
  • Experiment knowledge base for institutional learning
LATER
The Next Gen
  • AI-powered experiment insights and anomaly detection
  • Intelligent experiment planning based on historical data
  • Sophisticated holdout management at scale
  • Automated learning synthesis across the knowledge base
The sequencing logic

Each phase has a specific unlock condition. NOW builds trust - teams need to believe the platform works before they'll adopt it. NEXT removes friction - once trust exists, self-service removes the bottleneck on analysts. LATER adds intelligence - once the platform is running at scale, historical data becomes a strategic asset that AI can leverage. Skipping phases doesn't accelerate the roadmap; it undermines adoption.

Reflections

How I Approached This

  • Open with a hook, not a framework. The "spot the difference" game was a deliberate choice. PMs who open with slides full of 2×2 matrices lose their audience before the first framework lands. A concrete, unexpected experience creates curiosity - and curiosity is what makes the rest of the deck land.
  • Pillars before roadmap. I deliberately structured the presentation with the three pillars before the NOW/NEXT/LATER roadmap. A roadmap without a strategic anchor is just a feature list. The pillars give the roadmap meaning - every phase decision traces back to one of the three pillars it unlocks or strengthens.
  • Measurement for every pillar. "Simplicity" and "reliability" are empty words without a way to evaluate them. Attaching a concrete metric to each pillar transforms them from values into commitments - and makes it possible to have an honest conversation about whether you're achieving them.
  • Phase sequencing is as important as phase content. The hardest part of a platform roadmap isn't knowing what to build - it's knowing why to build it in a particular order. I spent more time on the sequencing logic (trust → adoption → intelligence) than on the individual feature lists within each phase.
